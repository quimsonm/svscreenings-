# Sample application — ultrasound newsletter piece

This is a demonstration of the skill applied to a totally different context (ultrasound business) to show that the voice mechanics transfer.

---

## The First Time You Really Saw Them

It starts with a flutter.

You're washing dishes, or stuck in traffic, or halfway through a work meeting you didn't want to be in. And something moves. A little wave from the inside. It's faint. It's quick. It's easy to miss if you're not paying attention.

And then it's gone.

You put your hand on your belly and wait. Nothing. Maybe you imagined it. Maybe it was your coffee. But deep down, you know what it was. Your baby just said hello for the first time — and nobody else in the room heard it.

That's how a lot of pregnancy goes, doesn't it? You carry most of it alone. The first positive test. The first nausea. The first sleepless night wondering if you're doing this right. Your partner's excited. Your mom's excited. Your sister texts you a belly emoji every Tuesday. But the little human you're carrying? You're the only one who actually gets to feel them in there.

And you love that.

And it's also a lot.

When my wife was pregnant with our first, she'd come home from work drained, put her hand on her belly, and say, "He moved today." And I'd smile. I'd say something like, "That's awesome, babe." But I didn't *get* it. Not really. Because I hadn't seen him yet. He was still a theory to me. A grainy black-and-white blob from an eight-week appointment and a heartbeat I'd heard through a speaker that sounded like a washing machine.

Then we went in for our first 3D scan. The lights dimmed. The wand moved. And there he was. His little nose. His hand by his face, like he was thinking hard about something. My wife started crying. I started crying. And for the first time in six months — I actually saw my son.

That was the day it stopped being her pregnancy. And started being our baby.

Here's what I've learned running this studio: most moms don't just come in because they want a cute picture to post. They come in because they want their people to finally *see* what they've been feeling for months. The husband who says the right things but doesn't fully get it. The grandparents who live four states away. The big sister who keeps asking when her baby is coming.

You want them in the room with you. You want them to feel it too.

So, mama — if you've been waiting for the "right time" to book that session, let me ask you something:

What are you waiting for?

The flutter is already happening. The nose is already there. The little hand is already curled up next to their face, waiting for someone to notice. You don't have to carry this alone anymore.

Come in. Bring your people. Let them see what you've been feeling.

We'll take care of the rest.

## Voice checklist verification

1. **Opens with a scene/question/image?** Yes — "It starts with a flutter."
2. **Single-line paragraphs as pivots?** Yes, multiple ("And then it's gone." / "And you love that." / "And it's also a lot.")
3. **Triadic list with parallel structure?** Yes — "You're washing dishes, or stuck in traffic, or halfway through a work meeting…"
4. **Concrete visceral metaphor?** Yes — washing machine for the heartbeat, "still a theory to me"
5. **Confessional I-voice?** Yes — the husband-confessional mid-piece
6. **Long sentence + short fragments?** Yes — "She'd come home from work drained, put her hand on her belly, and say 'He moved today.' And I'd smile. I'd say something like, 'That's awesome, babe.'"
7. **Ends on an invitation/question?** Yes — "What are you waiting for?" and "Come in. Bring your people. Let them see what you've been feeling."
8. **No random brand-site sentences?** Clean.
9. **No "journey," "experience," "leverage"?** Clean.
10. **Sounds like a person across a table?** Yes.

All ten pass. This is a working application of the skill to a non-religious, non-men's-content context.
