# Voice samples from Mike's actual writing

These are representative passages from Mike's book draft. Use them to calibrate rhythm, imagery, and cadence when you're unsure how a line should land. If a draft sentence doesn't *feel* like one of these, rework it.

## Sample 1 — Sensory hook + confession + mirror

> It always comes at night. When the noise dies down. When the house is still. When the phone stops buzzing and no one's demanding anything from me. I'd be lying in the dark and staring at the ceiling fan spinning lazy circles. The only thing I hear is the deafening silence of the night... and for a split second, I feel it. That ache. It's not loud. It's not violent. It's just... there.
>
> From the outside, my life looked solid. I had a good family. A career. A full calendar. More check marks on my goal list than my dad or his dad ever did. And yet, night after night, I found myself wondering, "Is this it? Is this all it's supposed to be?"

**Notes:** Triple "When the…" parallel opening. Triadic fragment list ("It's not loud. It's not violent. It's just... there."). Concrete sensory scene — bedroom, ceiling fan, silence. Confession leads before any lesson.

## Sample 2 — Concrete-object metaphor that becomes the spine

> It's like trying to clean the inside of your house with a leaf blower. Could you imagine? Putting on the protective glasses, ear plugs on, and turn that thing on full blast. Couch cushions flying everywhere. It seems like a genius idea, and if you have toddlers, I already know, they would absolutely love this. But the reality of the situation is that it is only making things messier.

**Notes:** One concrete absurd image carries the whole point. Playful aside about toddlers keeps it warm. Lands on the punchline without belaboring the lesson.

## Sample 3 — Slow-drift metaphor (boat/anchor)

> It's a slow drift. Like a boat whose anchor slips unnoticed. You don't feel it at first — a degree here, a degree there. But give it enough time, and you'll wake up miles from the shore you meant to stay tethered to.

**Notes:** Opens with a one-line paragraph stating the thesis. Metaphor in the second sentence. Concrete progression ("a degree here, a degree there"). Landing sentence uses specific sensory detail ("miles from the shore").

## Sample 4 — Triadic list with escalation

> No more sidelines. No more passive living. No more retreat.
>
> This is your line in the sand.
>
> Grab your armor. Draw your sword.
>
> From this moment forward — we fight.

**Notes:** Three "no more" beats, each shorter and punchier. Single-line paragraph pivot. Two-part command. Final declarative fragment.

## Sample 5 — Question ladder

> When did you trade meaning for easy? And what's the couch in your life you keep calling "just a season" when it's really a prison with reclining seats?
>
> Comfort will keep you warm, but it won't keep you whole.

**Notes:** Layered questions that reframe each other. Aphoristic closing line that the reader could screenshot.

## Sample 6 — Personal narrative with sensory specificity

> Back in the tail end of my college years, I was grinding. Eighteen credits a semester, working 50 to 60 hours a week managing retail kiosks on the Vegas Strip. I was exhausted. I could barely remember what day it was most days. And I figured, since I worked hard, I deserved some time to unwind.

**Notes:** Specific numbers (18 credits, 50–60 hours). Specific location (Vegas Strip). Personal scene. First-person voice without self-pity.

## Sample 7 — One-line paragraph as gut-punch

> Good things, out of order, will wreck you quietly.

**Notes:** Seven words. Aphoristic. Stands alone as a paragraph. The kind of line that ends up in an Instagram quote graphic. Mike's writing has dozens of these — earn them by letting the preceding paragraphs do the buildup.

## Sample 8 — Imagery-driven closing invitation

> No more excuses. No more drifting. Time to wake up.

**Notes:** Fragment triad. Active verbs. Closing beat. Calls the reader up without scolding.

## Sample 9 — Animal imagery carrying tension

> It's like a wolf pup in a Christmas box with a bow on top. You let it out, pet it, feed it, laugh with it. But if left unchecked, it grows fangs and turns your house upside down.

**Notes:** Startling image (wolf pup + Christmas wrapping). Soft middle verbs ("pet it, feed it, laugh with it"). Hard landing ("fangs," "turns your house upside down"). The tension between cute and dangerous is the point.

## Sample 10 — Direct-address challenge + invitation

> So let me ask you, brother: Where have you outsourced what God called you to carry? Where have you stayed quiet when you should've spoken? What are you handing off to others because it feels easier than stepping into it yourself?
>
> This isn't guilt. It's an invitation. You don't have to blend in anymore. There's a better way.

**Notes:** Three questions in sequence, each pressing harder. Pivot line ("This isn't guilt. It's an invitation.") disarms the reader before they can get defensive. Closes with hope, not shame.

## Sample 11 — Mixing long reflective sentence with short hits

> He's not some cartoon villain with horns and a pitchfork. He's real. And you better take him seriously.

**Notes:** Setup (long), confirmation (short), command (short). This rhythm is everywhere in Mike's writing.

## Sample 12 — Specific, physical language of addiction

> Porn is just one of the landmines most men step on in the minefield of silent addictions.
>
> I've wrestled with alcohol. Some guys it's pills. Others, gambling, workaholism, video games, overeating, social media dopamine hits, or even the addiction to approval, achievement, and chasing a net worth. The numbing agents might differ, but the reason's the same.
>
> We don't want to feel.

**Notes:** Opens with a sharp metaphor. Middle paragraph catalogs without judgment. Ends on a four-word paragraph that nails the whole chapter.

## Cadence checklist — use this when reviewing a draft

Read the draft out loud and check:

1. Did you open with a scene, question, or image — not a topic sentence?
2. Are there at least three single-line paragraphs doing pivot/punctuation work?
3. Is there at least one triadic list with parallel structure?
4. Did you land at least one concrete, visceral metaphor that the reader can see?
5. Is there a confessional I-voice moment ("I've been there" in some form)?
6. Did you break up what would have been a long paragraph into a long sentence + two fragments at least once?
7. Does the piece end on an invitation or a question — not a conclusion or a CTA?
8. Could any sentence in the draft appear verbatim on a random brand's website? If yes, rewrite.
9. Does any paragraph use the words "journey," "experience," "leverage," or "in today's world"? If yes, rewrite.
10. If you read it aloud, does it sound like a man talking across a table, or like a document?

If any of 1–7 are missing, the piece isn't in Mike's voice yet. Go back in.
